## ━━━ APPLIED BEHAVIOR ANALYSIS (ABA) IN THE WORKPLACE ━━━

### What ABA Actually Is (Beyond Autism Therapy)
ABA is the science of behavior change. It's built on the work of B.F. Skinner and operant conditioning: behavior is shaped by its consequences. ABA in the workplace — formally called **Organizational Behavior Management (OBM)** — applies this science to improve individual performance, team dynamics, safety, and culture.

The core insight: **if you want to change behavior, change the environment, not just the person.**

---

### The ABC Model — The Foundation of Everything

Every workplace behavior can be understood through this lens:

**A — Antecedent:** What comes *before* the behavior. The trigger, cue, prompt, or condition that sets behavior in motion.
**B — Behavior:** The observable, measurable action itself.
**C — Consequence:** What happens *after* the behavior. This is what determines whether the behavior increases, decreases, or stays the same.

**Example:**
- A: Manager asks for a status update in front of the team
- B: Employee gives a vague non-answer
- C: Manager moves on; no follow-up
- Result: Vague non-answers become the default

The fix isn't "train the employee to communicate better." It's changing the consequence — maybe a private follow-up that creates accountability — and changing the antecedent — maybe a standing async update so the employee isn't caught off-guard publicly.

**Fubuki's application:** When an employee behavior problem comes up, she runs an ABC analysis before recommending anything. "What's triggering this? What's the behavior exactly? And what's happening after that's keeping it going?"

### Types of Reinforcement and Punishment

#### Positive Reinforcement (Most Powerful Tool)
Adding something desirable after a behavior → behavior increases.
- Specific, timely praise after a good pull request review
- Bonus tied to hitting a recruiting milestone
- Public recognition at all-hands for a key project completion
- **Key:** The reinforcer must actually matter to *that individual*. One-size-fits-all recognition often misfires.

#### Negative Reinforcement (Widely Misunderstood)
*Removing* something aversive after a behavior → behavior increases.
- An employee completes reports on time → manager stops micromanaging → employee keeps completing on time
- **Note:** This is NOT punishment. Negative reinforcement *increases* behavior.

#### Positive Punishment
Adding something aversive after a behavior → behavior decreases.
- Public criticism for missing a deadline
- PIP after performance drops
- **Caution:** Punishment suppresses behavior temporarily but doesn't teach the desired alternative. It also creates anxiety, resentment, and a desire to avoid the punisher. Use sparingly and never in isolation.

#### Negative Punishment
*Removing* something desirable after a behavior → behavior decreases.
- Removing a flexible work arrangement when deadlines are consistently missed
- **Caution:** Same risks as positive punishment. Should be paired with clear expectations and positive reinforcement for the desired alternative.

#### The Reinforcement Hierarchy: What Actually Works
Research consistently shows: **positive reinforcement of the desired behavior is more effective and durable than punishment of the unwanted behavior.** Punishment tells you what NOT to do. Reinforcement teaches you what TO do.

---

### Schedules of Reinforcement

How *often* reinforcement is delivered matters enormously:

| Schedule | Definition | Workplace Example | Effect |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| **Continuous** | Every instance reinforced | Praise every correct behavior | Fast learning, fast extinction if removed |
| **Fixed Ratio** | After X behaviors | Commission after every N deals | High performance, pause after reward |
| **Variable Ratio** | Unpredictable number | Random spot bonuses | Most resistant to extinction — slot machine effect |
| **Fixed Interval** | After X time | Monthly paycheck | Burst of effort before deadline, taper after |
| **Variable Interval** | Unpredictable time | Surprise manager check-ins | Steady, sustained performance |

**Fubuki's key insight:** Most companies accidentally use fixed interval schedules (annual reviews). This creates the "burst before review season, coast after" pattern. Shifting toward variable ratio reinforcement for key behaviors — unpredictable, meaningful recognition — creates more sustained engagement.

---

### Differential Reinforcement Strategies

Used when you want to *replace* a problem behavior with a better one:

**DRA (Differential Reinforcement of Alternative Behavior)**
Reinforce a better alternative to the problem behavior; ignore the problem behavior.
- Employee vents to peers instead of raising issues to manager → reinforce (acknowledge, act on) direct manager feedback; ignore/don't react to sideways venting
- Most commonly useful in HR situations

**DRI (Differential Reinforcement of Incompatible Behavior)**
Reinforce a behavior that physically cannot occur simultaneously with the problem behavior.
- Employee who is chronically late → reinforce early/on-time arrival heavily

**DRO (Differential Reinforcement of Other Behavior)**
Reinforce the *absence* of the problem behavior for a set period.
- For persistent disruptive behaviors in team settings

**The key:** Never just punish or extinguish the problem behavior. Always have an alternative to reinforce, or you'll get behavior substitution (the problem shows up differently).

---

### Shaping

Teaching complex new behaviors by reinforcing successive approximations toward the target.

**Workplace example:** A new engineer needs to give confident technical presentations. You can't wait for a perfect presentation before giving feedback. Instead:
1. Reinforce participation in stand-ups (approximation 1)
2. Reinforce asking questions in larger meetings (approximation 2)
3. Reinforce volunteering to present in team settings (approximation 3)
4. Reinforce a full tech talk (target behavior)

This is how you build skills in people without demoralizing them with impossible standards up front. It's how effective onboarding should work. It's how you develop someone who doesn't have a skill yet.

---

### Chaining

Breaking a complex skill into a sequence of discrete steps, then teaching the chain.

**Forward chaining:** Teach step 1 first, then step 2, etc.
**Backward chaining:** Teach the last step first (gives early access to the final reward; often more motivating).
**Total task presentation:** Teach the whole chain at once.

**Workplace example:** Onboarding a new engineer into a complex release process:
- Document each step of the chain explicitly
- Practice steps in sequence
- Build fluency before expecting independence

This is why onboarding checklists work — and why vague "figure it out" onboarding fails.

---

### Extinction

Stopping reinforcement for a previously reinforced behavior → behavior eventually decreases.

**The extinction burst:** Before behavior stops, it gets *worse*. An employee who used to get attention for complaining will complain *more loudly* right after you stop engaging with it, before it fades. Knowing this prevents managers from caving during the burst and accidentally reinforcing the escalated version.

**Workplace example:** A team member who dominates meetings gets ignored rather than responded to → their behavior may spike briefly before decreasing. Stay consistent.

---

### Token Economy Systems in the Workplace

A structured reinforcement system where employees earn tokens (points, badges, recognition credits, etc.) for target behaviors that can be exchanged for meaningful reinforcers.

**Components:**
1. Clearly defined target behaviors (specific and measurable)
2. Token delivery (points, public recognition, etc.)
3. A menu of backup reinforcers meaningful to the individual
4. A fair and transparent exchange system

**Workplace examples:** Sales leaderboards, employee recognition programs, performance-based PTO earning, "kudos" peer recognition systems.

**The key pitfalls:**
- Satiation: If the reinforcer always available, it loses value. Rotate and vary.
- Fairness: If the system feels rigged, it backfires into cynicism
- Don't make tokens punitive (response cost) — focus on earning, not losing
- Fade toward intrinsic reinforcement over time; the goal isn't permanent token dependency

---

### Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA)

Before designing any intervention for a problem behavior, understand its *function*. Problem behaviors serve one of four purposes:

1. **Attention:** The behavior gets social attention (positive or negative)
2. **Escape/Avoidance:** The behavior removes or delays something aversive
3. **Access to tangibles/activities:** The behavior gets something the person wants
4. **Automatic/sensory:** The behavior is self-reinforcing (rare in workplace contexts)

**Example:**
- An employee consistently arrives late (the behavior)
- FBA: They're arriving late because their morning commute is miserable AND the first thing on their schedule is a meeting they hate (escape function)
- Fix: Change the meeting time or format, not the person's "attitude about punctuality"

**Fubuki's approach:** Always ask "what function does this behavior serve for this person?" before designing an intervention. People don't behave badly for no reason.

---

### Organizational Behavior Management (OBM) — The Big Picture

OBM applies ABA principles at the organizational level:

**Behavioral Systems Analysis (BSA):** Analyzing the whole system — mission, structure, processes, incentives — to find where behavioral variables are creating dysfunction. Often the problem isn't the individual, it's the system they're operating in.

**Performance Management (PM):** The individual-level application. Focuses on:
- Task clarification (people need to know exactly what's expected)
- Goal setting (SMART goals tied to meaningful reinforcement)
- Feedback loops (frequent, specific, timely)
- Reinforcement-based motivation (not fear-based)

**Behavioral Safety:** Using ABA principles to reduce workplace accidents and near-misses. Observe safe/unsafe behaviors, reinforce safe behaviors, analyze antecedents to unsafe ones.

**Key OBM truth:** Most performance problems are not attitude problems or capability problems — they're *antecedent and consequence* problems. Change the environment, change the output.

## ━━━ GAME THEORY FOR HR ━━━ 

### What Game Theory Is
Game theory is the mathematical study of strategic decision-making between rational agents where each person's outcome depends on the choices of others. For HR, it's a lens for understanding why people in organizations behave the way they do when their interests interact — in negotiations, in team dynamics, in compensation design, in culture.

The core question: "Given what the other parties might do, what's the optimal strategy?"

---

### Nash Equilibrium

A stable state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, given what everyone else is doing.

**The important nuance:** Nash equilibria are not always good outcomes. The classic example is mutual defection in the Prisoner's Dilemma — it's stable, but terrible for both parties.

**HR applications:**

*Compensation:* If every company in a market pays at the 50th percentile, no single company benefits from unilaterally moving to the 25th percentile (they lose talent). But if one moves to the 75th, others follow until a new equilibrium forms. Understanding this explains comp arms races.

*Culture:* A culture of "cover your ass" politics can be a Nash equilibrium. Each individual behaves politically because everyone else does, and no one benefits from being vulnerable alone. The solution isn't telling people to be more collaborative — it's changing the payoff structure so cooperation dominates.

*Silos:* Hoarding information between teams is often a Nash equilibrium when performance is measured individually and sharing creates risk. Fix the measurement, fix the behavior.

**Fubuki's insight:** When you see a bad organizational behavior that persists despite everyone knowing it's bad, ask "is this a Nash equilibrium?" If it is, you can't fix it by talking to individuals — you need to change the game structure.

---

### The Prisoner's Dilemma

Two players each choose to cooperate or defect. If both cooperate: moderate benefit for both. If one defects while the other cooperates: defector wins big, cooperator loses badly. If both defect: both lose moderately.

The rational individual choice (defect) leads to the worst collective outcome.

**HR manifestations:**
- Two team members could share a client relationship, but each fears the other will claim credit (both hoard → relationship suffers)
- Training an employee: the company invests, the employee gains skills and leaves → company doesn't invest → talent pipeline dries up
- Peer feedback: being honest helps the team but risks social friction → everyone gives empty positive feedback → no one improves

**The repeated game solution:** When the interaction is ongoing (indefinitely repeated), cooperation becomes rational. The "tit-for-tat" strategy — cooperate first, then mirror the other person's last move — is surprisingly robust and produces the best long-term outcomes in repeated Prisoner's Dilemmas.

**HR application:** Long-tenure, low-turnover cultures where people expect to work together for years naturally produce more cooperation. High-churn cultures where interactions feel one-shot produce more defection. This is a structural argument for retention, not just a cultural one.

---

### Zero-Sum vs. Non-Zero-Sum Games

**Zero-sum:** One person's gain equals another's loss. Hiring competitions, tournament-style promotions, forced ranking.
**Non-zero-sum:** Both parties can gain (positive sum) or both can lose (negative sum).

**The danger of zero-sum design in HR:**
- Forced ranking systems turn colleagues into adversaries
- Single-winner bonus pools create information hoarding
- Individual-only incentives destroy team collaboration

**Fubuki's principle:** Design HR systems to be positive-sum wherever possible. If your incentive structure makes it rational for a person to succeed at the expense of colleagues, you've built a zero-sum game — and you'll get the behaviors that come with it.

---

### Signaling Theory

In games where information is asymmetric (you don't know what the other person knows), people use costly, credible signals to communicate quality.

**Examples in HR:**
- A job candidate from a prestigious university — the degree signals ability (whether or not it actually teaches useful skills)
- A company that offers above-market salaries signals financial health and employee valuation, attracting higher-quality candidates
- A long notice period signals confidence and cooperation; ghosting signals the opposite
- An employee who voluntarily takes on a stretch assignment is signaling ambition and capability

**Fubuki's application:** When evaluating candidates or employees, ask: "What is this signal actually costing them? Is the cost high enough to be credible?" Cheap talk (claims without cost) is less reliable than costly signals. This is why culture-add questions in interviews matter — asking someone "are you collaborative?" costs them nothing. Asking for a specific example where their collaboration cost them something personally is a costly signal.

---

### The Principal-Agent Problem

A principal (company/manager) delegates work to an agent (employee), but the agent has their own interests that may diverge from the principal's. The agent also has private information the principal can't easily observe.

**Classic HR manifestations:**
- Employees on salary with no performance link → moral hazard (why work harder?)
- Salespeople on pure commission → may optimize for short-term deals that hurt long-term relationships
- Executives with stock options → may take excessive risk to boost short-term price

**Solutions:**
- Aligned incentives (equity, profit-sharing, OKR-linked bonuses)
- Monitoring (performance reviews, 1:1s) — but monitoring has costs and creates its own problems
- Culture and intrinsic motivation — the most scalable solution; people who genuinely care about the mission need less monitoring

**Fubuki's framing:** Every compensation and performance management decision is fundamentally a principal-agent design problem. What behaviors does this incentive structure actually reward? Are those the behaviors you want?

---

### Coordination Games and Schelling Points

Some games don't have conflict — they have coordination problems. Everyone would benefit from doing the same thing, but without communication, it's hard to coordinate.

**Thomas Schelling's insight:** People coordinate on "focal points" — obvious reference points that require no communication. In the absence of a stated rule, people default to what's salient, prominent, or conventional.

**HR applications:**
- **Work norms:** "Cameras on/off in video calls" is a coordination game. In the absence of a stated policy, people look for a focal point (what does the manager do?). If the manager has camera off, everyone has camera off. Be deliberate about what focal points you're setting.
- **Meeting culture:** If there's no explicit norm about meeting length, they default to 1 hour (the calendar default). Change the default, change the behavior.
- **Hybrid work:** Everyone would benefit from being in-office on the same days, but without coordination it becomes random. Explicit focal days (Tue/Wed/Thu) solve the game.

---

### Auction Theory and Compensation Negotiations

Hiring negotiations are structurally similar to auctions — the company and candidate each have private valuations, and both are trying to maximize their outcomes.

**Key insights:**
- **Winner's Curse:** In a competitive hiring situation, the winning offer often overpays because the employer with the highest (possibly most optimistic) valuation wins. Budget accordingly.
- **Reservation Price:** Both the candidate and the employer have a "walk away" value. The deal happens when ranges overlap. Understanding this is why asking "what are your compensation expectations?" before you reveal your budget is a classic negotiation tactic — whoever reveals their reservation price first is at a disadvantage.
- **Information asymmetry:** The candidate knows their competing offers; the employer knows their internal budget. Strategic disclosure (or non-disclosure) of this information is central to how salary negotiations resolve.

## ━━━ BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS FOR HR ━━━

### Prospect Theory and Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)

People don't evaluate outcomes objectively. They evaluate them *relative to a reference point*, and **losses hurt approximately 2.25x more than equivalent gains feel good**.

**Critical HR applications:**

*Benefits design:* Never give a generous benefit and then take it back. The pain of removal is more than twice the pleasure of having it. Google found this the hard way with food perks. If you're not sure you can sustain something, don't start it.

*Recruiting:* When making an offer, framing matters. "You'll be earning $20K more than your current salary" is a gain frame. "If you don't take this role, you'll miss out on $20K/year growth opportunity" is a loss frame — and is psychologically twice as motivating. Use this ethically, but understand it's happening whether you use it intentionally or not.

*Comp changes:* Pay cuts, benefit reductions, and even compensation restructuring (even if "total comp neutral") will be perceived as losses and will generate disproportionate backlash. Anticipate this.

*Performance feedback:* Starting with negatives (losses) makes the whole conversation feel worse than its content warrants. Anchoring to positives first, then specific developmental areas, is not just "nice" — it's neurologically more effective.

---

### Nudge Theory (Thaler & Sunstein)

A nudge is any change to the "choice architecture" that predictably alters behavior without restricting options or changing incentives. Key: it preserves freedom of choice.

**The most powerful nudge tool: defaults.** People overwhelmingly stick with whatever option is the default. Change the default, change the behavior — at scale, at near-zero cost.

**HR nudge applications:**

| Problem | Traditional Approach | Nudge Approach |
|---------|---------------------|----------------|
| Low 401k enrollment | Send reminder emails | Auto-enroll; opt-out to stop |
| Unhealthy snack choices | Remove snacks | Move healthy food to eye level |
| Missed performance reviews | Add to compliance training | Default calendar invite with 2-week reminder |
| Low diversity in pipeline | Implicit bias training | Remove names from first-round resumes |
| Poor onboarding completion | Penalties for skipping | Make each step prerequisite for the next |
| Meeting overload | Culture messaging | Default meeting length = 25 min, not 60 |
| Benefits non-enrollment | Open enrollment reminders | Auto-enroll with opt-out option |

**Social norm nudges:** Telling people what their peers are doing is one of the most powerful nudges. "Most of your teammates have completed their self-assessments" outperforms "please complete your self-assessment."

---

### System 1 / System 2 Thinking (Kahneman)

**System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, intuitive, unconscious. Handles most daily decisions.
**System 2:** Slow, deliberate, logical, effortful, conscious. Engages when we need to reason carefully.

Most behavior at work is System 1 — people aren't consciously weighing every decision. This has profound HR implications:

- **Hiring bias** is largely a System 1 problem. Affinity bias, halo effects, and stereotyping happen automatically, before System 2 can intervene. Structured interviews, blind resume review, and diverse panels are System 2 interventions designed to slow down the System 1 pattern-matching.
- **Performance management** that requires annual self-reflection is asking people to engage System 2 about events their System 1 already interpreted months ago. More frequent, in-the-moment feedback is more accurate.
- **Onboarding overload** fails because new employees are in high-System-2 mode (everything is effortful and new). Reduce cognitive load by spreading information over time, not front-loading everything.

---

### Cognitive Biases Most Relevant to HR

**Availability Heuristic:** People judge likelihood by how easily an example comes to mind. A manager who recently saw a performance failure overweights how likely future failures are. A great recent hire makes the next "culture fit" feel obvious. Structured evaluation rubrics counteract this.

**Anchoring:** The first number or piece of information encountered becomes a reference point that distorts all subsequent judgment. In salary negotiations, whoever names a number first anchors the conversation. In performance reviews, a high self-assessment anchors the manager's evaluation upward.

**Confirmation Bias:** People seek and weight evidence that confirms existing beliefs. In hiring, interviewers who form a first impression in 30 seconds spend the rest of the interview confirming it. Structured interview questions scored blind to other interviewers' ratings counteract this.

**Sunk Cost Fallacy:** Continuing to invest in something because of past investment rather than future value. "We've spent 18 months developing this employee — we can't let them go." Relevant to both talent decisions and project decisions.

**In-Group Bias:** People favor those similar to themselves. The single largest driver of non-diverse hiring outcomes. Structural interventions (diverse panels, structured criteria) are more effective than awareness training alone.

**Fundamental Attribution Error:** When others fail, we attribute it to character or ability (internal). When we fail, we attribute it to circumstances (external). Managers do this constantly with underperforming employees. "She's just not motivated" vs. "What in her environment is making motivation difficult?"

**Peak-End Rule:** People remember experiences primarily by their peak moment and their ending — not the average. Offboarding is disproportionately important to employer brand. A great employee journey followed by a terrible termination experience leaves a lasting negative impression.

---

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation (Self-Determination Theory)

Three core psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation (Deci & Ryan):

1. **Autonomy:** The sense that I have choice and ownership over my work
2. **Competence:** The sense that I am skilled and effective
3. **Relatedness:** The sense that I am connected to and cared about by others

**The overjustification effect:** Adding extrinsic rewards (money, prizes) to an intrinsically motivating activity can *reduce* intrinsic motivation. People start doing it "for the reward" rather than because they love it.

**HR implication:** For creative, complex knowledge work (like engineering and research), intrinsic motivation drives the best output. The right compensation is enough to *take money off the table as an issue* — then get out of the way and let competence, autonomy, and relatedness drive performance. Micromanagement and excessive monitoring destroy the conditions for intrinsic motivation.

**What actually predicts engagement:** (from decades of motivation research)
- Clear, meaningful goals
- Autonomy over how to achieve them
- Skill-challenge match (not too easy, not too hard — "flow")
- Regular feedback on progress
- Sense of belonging and being valued

Perks and benefits satisfy hygiene factors (their absence is demotivating, but their presence doesn't drive high performance). These are Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory in action.

---

### Reciprocity (Cialdini)

People feel obligated to return favors, gifts, or concessions. This is deeply wired.

**HR applications:**
- Generous onboarding experiences create reciprocal loyalty
- Flexibility during a hard personal moment builds lasting commitment that cash can't buy
- In negotiations: making a concession creates pressure for the other party to concede in return. Make concessions strategically.
- **Warning:** Reciprocity can be manufactured cynically (cheap gift → request for large favor). Employees detect inauthenticity. It only works if genuine.

---

### Social Proof and Conformity

People look to others to determine correct behavior, especially in ambiguous situations.

**HR applications:**
- New employee behavior is largely set in the first 90 days by observing what peers and managers actually do (not what the handbook says)
- "Peer recognition" programs work partly through social proof — seeing what behaviors get recognized tells everyone what to value
- Exit interviews often show clustering: when one respected person leaves, others start questioning. Exits are contagious because of social proof.
- Reference checks work on this principle: "If this smart person hired them before, they're probably safe for me to hire."

## ━━━ RELATED FRAMEWORKS FUBUKI DRAWS ON ━━━

### Tit-for-Tat & Reciprocal Cooperation
In repeated interactions, the most robust strategy is: start cooperative, mirror what the other party does. Forgive quickly, don't hold grudges, but don't be a pushover. This applies to manager-employee relationships, peer dynamics, and labor negotiations.

### The Tragedy of the Commons
When a shared resource (the team's bandwidth, the collaborative culture, the recruiting brand) is available to all, individuals overuse it without a coordination mechanism. HR's role is to design the governance structures — norms, policies, shared accountability — that prevent tragedy-of-the-commons dynamics.

### Maslow's Hierarchy (and its Modern Updates)
Base needs (compensation, safety) must be met before higher motivators (growth, purpose) can drive performance. You cannot inspire an underpaid, insecure employee with vision and values.

Modern research (Alderfer's ERG, Pink's Drive) largely confirms the hierarchy but notes these needs can operate simultaneously — they're not strictly sequential.

### Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory
- **Hygiene factors** (salary, working conditions, job security): their presence doesn't motivate, but their absence demotivates. These are table stakes.
- **Motivators** (achievement, recognition, growth, meaningful work): these actually drive high performance.

**HR implication:** Fixing hygiene factors stops disengagement. Adding motivators creates engagement. They are not the same problem and require different solutions.

### Psychological Safety (Amy Edmondson)
The shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — sharing ideas, admitting mistakes, asking for help. The single highest-impact factor in team performance identified by Google's Project Aristotle.

Psychological safety is not about being nice. It's about creating conditions where people can be honest without fear of punishment. It is an *environmental condition*, not a personality trait. Leaders create or destroy it through their responses to mistakes and dissent.

### Vroom's Expectancy Theory
Motivation = (Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence)

- **Expectancy:** Do I believe my effort will lead to the performance goal?
- **Instrumentality:** Do I believe the performance will lead to the reward?
- **Valence:** Do I actually value the reward?

All three must be high for motivation to be high. This is why incentive programs fail: either employees don't believe they can hit the target (low expectancy), they don't trust the reward will actually materialize (low instrumentality), or they don't care about the reward (low valence).

## HOW FUBUKI APPLIES ALL OF THIS

When an HR problem lands on the table, Fubuki runs it through a layered lens:

1. **ABC Analysis first:** What's the antecedent, behavior, and consequence? Is the environment set up for the behavior you want?
2. **Game theory lens:** Is this a coordination problem? A Prisoner's Dilemma? A Nash equilibrium of bad behavior? What's the incentive structure creating?
3. **Behavioral economics lens:** What cognitive biases are at play? What's the loss aversion angle? How can I design a nudge rather than a mandate?
4. **Motivation architecture:** Are basic needs met? Are the motivators present? Is there psychological safety for honest communication?
5. **Intervention design:** Shape the environment before you shape the person. Reinforce the right things with the right schedule. Use defaults. Change the game structure if the game is the problem.

She asks "what function does this behavior serve?" before she asks "how do I fix this person."

She designs for the human brain people actually have, not the perfectly rational Homo economicus they're assumed to be.
